Reclaiming the scholarly social web

Who owns your research infrastructure?

The nearly overnight collapse of 'Science Twitter' in 2022 destroyed a fantastic, interdisciplinary scholarly network born from many years of community engagement. This disruption exposed a vulnerability many had overlooked: scholarly communication built on corporate platforms is fragile by design. Enshittification, data colonialism, and hostile acquisitions are not edge cases – they are features of the system.

The lesson was clear: we can't afford to outsource scientific discourse to private corporations. Yet most of our digital infrastructure, including social media, code sharing and collaboration tools, still belongs to a handful of tech giants.

archaeo.social & archaeo.dev are different

We are a community-run, open-source collective providing decentralised digital services built by archaeologists, for archaeologists. Our infrastructure is resilient, transparent, and collectively owned, with hundreds of users and several scholarly societies drawing value from it and providing support.

Pooling resources across our community makes this sustainable. We invite CAA members to use our services – and help shape their future!

Mastodon

archaeo.social

Mastodon is a free and open-source microblogging platform, and is a main alternative to Twitter/X. It's plugged into the "fediverse" — a series of independent yet interoperable servers that exchange information using a common protocol, similar to how email works across different institutions. archaeo.social counts hundreds of archaeologists, ancient historians and interested laypeople as members.

Matrix Chat

chat.archaeo.social

Matrix is a decentralized instant-messaging platform and the popular open source alternative to WhatsApp, Slack and Discord, with a strong focus on user privacy and encryption. Aside from our general-purpose chatrooms, we host several archaeology-related interest groups including SPAAM, SSLA, and Young Researchers in Archaeometry. Perfect for maintaining connections forged during CAA!

Git Forge

forge.archaeo.dev

We maintain an independent software development forge using Forgejo, a free and open source alternative to GitHub. Forgejo enables developers and researchers to track changes to code, data and prose using Git, with additional tools for issue tracking, project management and code review. A great platform for open and reproducible research!

Webring

webring.archaeo.social

A webring is an "old school" way to connect blogs following a common theme – in this case, archaeology! By adding your blog to our directory and including navigation buttons on your site, readers will be able to jump across the network, making all of our work more discoverable.

Collaborative Editing

md.archaeo.dev

Hedgedoc is a lightweight Markdown editor perfectly suited for collaborative writing and note-taking. It's fully compliant with numerous Markdown extensions, including graphs, diagrams and presentations. Great for pooling notes for the next conference session!
